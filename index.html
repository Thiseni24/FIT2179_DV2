Act One

Where Cinema Comes From

Film production is global, but highly uneven. A small number of countries dominate output while many others contribute smaller yet still significant film industries.

The map shows that cinema production is geographically widespread, but only a few regions emerge as dominant production hubs.

The United States produces dramatically more films than every other country in the dataset, highlighting the scale difference between Hollywood and other national industries.

Act Two

How Cinema Changed Over Time

Film output and genre popularity have shifted across decades, reflecting changing audience preferences, technological change and global disruptions.

Film production increased steadily for decades before dropping sharply during the COVID-19 pandemic period.

Genres rise and fall in waves rather than growing evenly, suggesting that audience interest shifts over time.

Act Three

What Films Look and Sound Like

Films differ not only by country, but also by language, structure, audience attention and commercial scale.

English-language films dominate the dataset, though many other languages continue to maintain visible cinematic presence.

Each genre forms a distinct “fingerprint” based on runtime, rating, revenue, budget and audience review activity.

So, what does the world on screen look like?

Global cinema is shaped by concentration and diversity at the same time: a few countries and languages dominate production, while genres and audience interests continue to evolve across decades.
